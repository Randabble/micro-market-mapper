Understanding the Problem
ParkPal needs to answer one question: "Which specific neighborhoods should we launch in first?"
This is a cold-start marketplace problem. You need to find zones where:

Drivers are genuinely desperate for parking (demand exists and is unmet)
Homeowners with driveways are physically nearby (supply can exist)
Those two things overlap geographically within walking distance

The failure modes are:

Pure demand signal (downtown Manhattan) → no driveways nearby, useless
Pure supply signal (suburban sprawl) → nobody needs parking there
Both present but too far apart → a driveway 1.5 miles from a venue is worthless

The algorithm has to force all three conditions to be true simultaneously, not just correlated.

The Data
Here's exactly where to get each input, all free:
Demand side:

Parking citations → your city's open data portal (Seattle: data.seattle.gov, search "parking violations"; Bellevue: data.bellevue.wa.gov). Download as CSV. Each row is one ticket with lat/lon, date, time, violation type.
Points of interest / venues → OpenStreetMap Overpass API (free). Query for stadiums, arenas, transit hubs, office clusters within your target city.

Supply side:

Residential parcels → King County Assessor open data (kingcounty.gov/services/gis). Download parcel shapefile. Each polygon is one property with zoning code. Filter for SF (single family) zones — these are your potential hosts.
Driveway proxy → You won't get driveway data directly. Use residential parcel count as the proxy. Single-family parcels almost universally have driveways.

Competition:

Existing public parking → OpenStreetMap (tag: amenity=parking, access=public). Also city parking garage datasets on the same open data portals.


The Algorithm
Core Insight: Walkability-Constrained Overlap
The fundamental operation is: for each small geographic cell, measure how much parking desperation exists within walking distance, and how much residential supply exists within that same walking distance. If both are high in the same cell, that cell is a target.
Use a hexagonal grid (H3 library, resolution 9 ≈ 174m diameter hexagons) over your city. This gives you consistent, non-overlapping cells to score.

Step 1: Build the Demand Surface
For each hex cell, count parking citations that fall within it. But raw counts are noisy — a cell might have more tickets just because it's bigger or because enforcement is heavier there. So you need to control for that.
Demand score for cell i:
Di=CiAi×log⁡(1+Ei)D_i = \frac{C_i}{A_i} \times \log(1 + E_i)Di​=Ai​Ci​​×log(1+Ei​)

The log term prevents a single massive stadium from blowing up the score. You're looking for consistent demand pressure, not one-off anomalies.

Step 3: Penalize for Existing Competition
For each cell, measure how much public parking already exists nearby. If garages are abundant, your value proposition weakens.
Pi=public parking spots within 400m of cell iarea in km2P_i = \frac{\text{public parking spots within 400m of cell } i}{\text{area in km}^2}Pi​=area in km2public parking spots within 400m of cell i​
Convert this to a competition penalty between 0 and 1:
Penaltyi=Pimax⁡(P)+ϵ\text{Penalty}_i = \frac{P_i}{\max(P) + \epsilon}Penaltyi​=max(P)+ϵPi​​
You'll subtract a fraction of this from the final score.

Step 4: The Final Score — Geometric Mean with Penalty
This is the critical design decision. You use a geometric mean between demand and supply, not addition. This mathematically guarantees that if either side is zero, the score is zero — no false positives.
Alphai=Di×Si×(1−0.3×Penaltyi)\text{Alpha}_i = \sqrt{D_i \times S_i} \times (1 - 0.3 \times \text{Penalty}_i)Alphai​=Di​×Si​​×(1−0.3×Penaltyi​)
The 0.3 coefficient means existing competition can suppress your score by at most 30%. You don't zero out a zone just because a garage exists — garages are expensive and people hate them. But you do discount it.
The output is 0–100 for every hexagon in the city.

Step 5: Cluster and Rank
Individual high-scoring hexagons that are isolated aren't useful — you need contiguous zones with enough supply density to actually recruit multiple hosts.
Take all hexagons with Alpha > 60, then run DBSCAN clustering (density-based, no need to specify number of clusters) on their centroids. Each cluster becomes a "launch zone." Rank clusters by their mean Alpha score weighted by cluster size.
Your output is a ranked list: "Launch Zone 1: Wilburton neighborhood, 14 hexagons, avg score 74.2, estimated 340 driveable parcels within zone."

Why This Works
The geometric mean enforces the both-sides-must-exist constraint at the math level, not at the code logic level. DBSCAN finds natural contiguous clusters without you having to guess how many zones exist. Distance decay on the supply side bakes in the walking-distance reality — a driveway that's 900m away from the citation cluster doesn't count. The competition penalty is soft, not binary, because garages don't eliminate demand, they just reduce your margin.
